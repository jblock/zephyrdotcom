Start

Jan 25, 2013

Client Kick-off Meeting

On January 25, 2013, Boeing clients flew to our Pittsburgh campus to meet our team and kick start the project. This was the first time we got to meet our clients, and after some quick introductions we went straight to business. We discussed the scope of our project, which was to improve communication in the Everett Factory, and we discussed the needs of Boeing as well as the goals of the HCI Capstone project.

Jan - Feb 2013

Literature Review

To gain a more detailed understanding of the problem space of factory communication, we reviewed relevant literature. Some resources were academic journals grounded in theoretical solutions to communication and efficiency problems, while others were sourced from magazine articles and industry whitepapers describing success stories.

Jan - Feb 2013

Competitive Analysis

We further researched relevant product spaces to broaden our domain knowledge prior to conducting field research. We examined processes and tools that enable efficient factory communication in large-scale operations. In particular, we examined integration of factory data with Manufacturing Execution Systems, process improvement methodologies employed in factory settings, and next-generation airplane production paradigms.

Feb 2013

Gathering Data

We flew to Seattle twice in February to perform research on the factory floor. We interviewed 25 factory floor workers from 12 different roles and attended various meetings.

Contextual Interviews and Shadowing

Working with contacts at the factory, we set up on-the-job shadowing with Boeing employees. Our goal was to observe workflow as it occurred naturally, rather than have it described to us. This contextual experience accounts for the fact that someone’s perception or memory of how they do work can be different from what actually happens on the job. People often forget about repetitive and monotonous tasks that may in fact be central to their work. They also might recall an ideal, hypothetical version of a workflow rather than describe the reality of the situation. Contextual observation gave us a clear picture of work is being done at the factory.

Attending IT and Process Improvement Meetings

We attended a number of IT events, including Lean and Kaizen meetings, and extensively interviewed IT employees. This gave us an understanding of the factory from the perspective of the people who design and build its products and informed us of various initiatives that have been made to address workers’ needs.

Ethnography

Since communication was the broad focus of our research, any interaction we observed in the factory presented a potentially useful insight. From waiting in line at the factory’s coffee shop to watching our participants play ping-pong, we spent time in between interviews observing cultural trends and the factory floor as a whole. Throughout the entire experience, we looked for patterns of interaction and behavior that could inform the rest of our research findings.

Feb 2013

Interpretation

Once we arrived back in Pittsburgh, we held interpretation sessions to have an organized structure to our notes from our contextual interviews and to make sure everyone in our team knew of each other's interviews. In order to receive the greatest benefit from interviewing, we interpreted our notes from the interviews within 48 hours of their occurrence. This allowed for clearer recollection of facts and details, enriching the accuracy of our data.

We transmitted the information we learned individually to the group as a whole by telling the stories of our research experiences. The storyteller explained background information in order to form a collective understanding of the data.

Team members who were not present during the interview probed for more details and tried to understand the deeper motivations of observed behaviors. This led the interviewer to think and reflect on what happened and brought their personal insights to the surface.

Feb - Mar 2013

Data Synthesis

Affinity Diagramming

We created affinity diagrams of our notes created during our interpretation sessions. The process of affinity diagramming involved the creation of hierarchical categories from detailed data. We created categories on the basis of perceived similarity and connection between individual notes. Unconventional ways of grouping notes were encouraged, so as to interpret information in a way that fostered creative thinking.

Cultural Models

Creating cultural models helped reveal influences that impact how workers do their jobs. Cultural models visualize the often invisible influences on an individual, such as policies, pressures, and organizational trends. Understanding the culture of the Everett Factory was important for contextualizing and gaining an understanding of workers’ experiences.

Flow Models

We created flow models to capture the flow of people, actions, information, and artifacts from the perspective of one individual. Generating flow models illustrated successes and breakdowns in communication between workers. They served as visible documentation of a worker’s concrete responsibilities as they were enacted during interviews.

Mar - May 2013

Form-giving and Visioning

We envisioned potential solutions during collaborative storyboarding sessions, where we gave form to our ideas without judging their feasibility. We came out of these sessions with five clear product directions: improving communication between shifts, real-time mobile communication, collaboration between technicians and engineers, allowing all people's voices to be heard, and improving employee morale through involvement and appreciation.

May 6, 2013

Spring Presentation

As a way of updating our client on our progress and also gaining a clearer scope for our summer design phase, we presented our research process, findings, and visions to Boeing contacts. They showed enthusiasm for all product directions, but requested that we focus on social relationships, morale, and appreciation in order to make the biggest impact on Boeing culture in the short time that remained.

May - Jun 2013

Brainstorming and Ideation

After we narrowed the scope of our project, we brainstormed 153 ideas to expand upon our two socially-oriented visions. Again, we focused on not judging ideas as we came up with them. Even an impossible idea can spark conversation that leads to a powerful product concept. After showing our ideas to classmates, we narrowed down our ideas into seven concepts.

Jun 2013

Storyboarding and Speed-dating

Speed dating is a testing technique in which you show a number of ideas to each participant, getting quick reactions to each one. We sketched our 7 concepts into cohesive storyboards, and showed them to 42 employees at the factory. This round of testing narrowed down our focus to the 3 most well-received concepts: Scream Box, Art Station, and Pat on the Back.

Jun 2013

Low-fidelity and Paper Prototyping

We found that the needs addressed in our final 3 concepts were interconnected and part of a larger story, a concept which we called "The Vent." We created a service design model for employees to express themselves, be heard, and feel appreciated. The Scream Box, Art Station, and Feedbaack Board were prototyped as three touchpoints in The Vent, a break room for employees to use when they have free time. We tested this experience with 14 Boeing employees at the factory.

Jul 2013

Final Prototype Development and Testing

Based on feedback we received on The Vent, we decided to further unify our concept of a room so that it felt like a truly cohesive experience. Our final working prototype was Solace, an interactive room where employees can express themselves by creating immersive audiovisual environments through basic interactions. We used a wizard-of-oz technique to simulate a high-fidelity version of this room, and iterated 4 times over the course of a full week of testing on 13 participants.
